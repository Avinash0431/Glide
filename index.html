Product Thinking · 6 Trade-offs How we decided what to build

The calls I made, and why. Why Glide is built the way it is.

Decision 01
Community before features
2.4×D30 retention uplift for users who post in their first 7 days. Community is the acquisition flywheel, retention engine, and data moat, not just a tab.Travelers trust people over platforms. Every post brings more people to Glide.
Network EffectConfidence: Medium
Unknown: the 2.4× signal is from analogous social products, not Glide. Risk: cold-start, an empty community reads as a dead product. Validate: D7 contribution rate in beta before scaling spend.
Decision 02
Visa AI in Phase 1, not Phase 3
18moTo replicate embassy-data partnerships. Dataset compounds with every application. Delaying hands competitors a head start that can't be recovered.The scariest part of any international trip, and no app has truly solved it. Yet.
Data MoatConfidence: High
Evidence: 7/20 interviews raised visa fear unprompted. Risk: a wrong approval estimate is catastrophic + legal. Validate: backtest model to >90% precision; ship as guidance, not guarantee.
Decision 03
RICE killed 14 features
14Features cut before Sprint 1, cruise, ferry, events, currency, loyalty. Each freed 0.5 sprints for the 6 features that define the moat.6 excellent features ship faster and better than 20 mediocre ones. We chose depth over breadth.
RICE ScopeConfidence: High

Decision 04
Absorb global rail cost now
5 APIsJR East · DB · Eurostar · Amtrak · Renfe. 12 to 18 months to replicate. We absorb the partnership cost. The moat is the return.JR Pass, Deutsche Bahn, Eurostar, all separate portals today. Glide puts them in one search.
Supply-Side MoatConfidence: Medium

Decision 05
Real price from search, always

Trust → LTVConfidence: High

Visa Assist didn't start here.

The final screen looks obvious. The first two versions weren't. Each failed test taught the next, this is the part of product work that doesn't show up in a portfolio screenshot.

V1 · Cut
A document checklist
First instinct: list every required document with upload slots. Logical, complete, thorough.
Why it failed: 4/5 testers still asked "but will I actually get approved?" A checklist answered what to submit, not the fear underneath, which was rejection.
V2 · Improved
A rules engine
Added eligibility logic: answer questions, get a yes/no with the rule cited. More honest, more useful.
Why it improved but missed: binary answers felt cold and absolute. Travel is probabilistic, a hard "No" lost trust when reality is "likely, with caveats."
V3 · Shipped
An approval probability
A confidence score (94%) plus the two factors moving it. Names the fear, quantifies it, stays honest about uncertainty.
Why it works: it speaks to the emotion (anxiety), not just the task (paperwork). Tested calmer and more trusted, and the score became the acquisition hook.
Decisions I Didn't Make

What I deliberately said no to.What didn't make the cut, on purpose.

Scope is defined by what you reject. I cut breadth before Sprint 1 so the MVP could answer one question cleanly: will travellers trust Glide enough to move from plan to booking?A good product isn't everything you can build. It's the few things you choose to build well.

AI Itinerary Generator cut v1
Shipped as a simple planner, not a generative agent. Full AI didn't solve the biggest pain (fragmentation) and added cost + latency risk. Sequenced to Phase 2.
Loyalty Programme rejected
Retention tool applied to an acquisition-stage product. You can't reward repeat behaviour you don't have yet. Wrong stage.
Social / Activity Feed rejected
Community must come before entertainment. A feed without trusted contributors is noise. Build utility-driven UGC first.
Dark Mode deferred
Zero impact on business metrics at this stage. Real engineering cost, no movement on activation or conversion. Later.
Cruise & Ferry Booking cut
Long-tail supply, tiny demand in target segments. RICE reach too low to justify integration complexity.
In-app Currency Wallet cut
Regulatory + fintech overhead for a problem already solved by display-only FX. Not worth the licensing burden.
Events & Ticketing cut
Adjacent, not core. Dilutes the "complete the journey" focus. Partnership candidate post-PMF, not a build.
Multi-currency Pricing Engine deferred
Display real total in local currency, yes. A full pricing engine, premature optimisation before scale demands it.
Influencer Marketplace rejected
Monetisation play before the audience exists. Inverts the flywheel. Earn the community first.
Native Web3 / Crypto Pay rejected
Solves no validated user pain. Tech looking for a problem. No interview ever asked for it.
Group-Split Payments deferred
Real pain for the family segment, but not the primary ICP for launch. Revisit when group bookings hit volume.
Offline Maps Engine buy not build
No edge in rebuilding maps. License it. Engineering focus belongs on Visa + Rail + Trip Hub.
North Star · NSM + AARRR FrameworkSuccess Metrics

MAB over MAU. Always.How we measure what matters.

NSM = Monthly Activated Bookers: users who complete one high-intent planning action and one booking action in the same trip workspace. Opening the app is not success. Moving from uncertainty to commitment is. We measure whether people actually book, not just whether they open the app.

Why MAB and not MAU? MAU rewards passive browsing. MAB counts users who complete a visa-readiness check, save a trip budget, and book or reserve at least one core item. The layers: Leading metrics (NPS, activation) tell us early; lagging metrics (GMV, repeat rate) confirm late; guardrails (rating, churn) stop us shipping growth that quietly breaks the product. Failure criterion: if activation <15% by Month 3, the consolidation thesis is wrong, and we stop, not push harder.

Intellectual Honesty

What could kill this.Where it might go wrong.

Every assumption is a bet. A senior PM names the bets that could break, and has a mitigation and a validation plan for each, before they're forced to.No product is guaranteed. Here's an honest look at what could go wrong, and the plan for each.

High Risk
Assumption: Rail/visa partnerships will close in time
If it fails
Phase 1 differentiation collapses to "another OTA." The moat trio never ships, and Glide competes on price.
Mitigation
Start BD before Sprint 1. Launch with 1 to 2 anchor rail partners (JR East) rather than all five. Sequence visa by top corridors.
Validation: Signed LOIs with 2 rail partners before committing Phase 1 scope. Gate the launch on partnership milestones, not calendar.
High Risk
Assumption: Visa AI will be accurate enough to trust
If it fails
A wrong approval estimate causes a rejected trip, catastrophic for trust and a potential legal exposure.
Mitigation
Ship as "guidance, not guarantee" with confidence ranges. Human-reviewed for high-stakes corridors. Conservative thresholds at launch.
Validation: Backtest model against 12 months of historical outcomes. Require >90% precision before surfacing a score to users.
Medium Risk
Assumption: Community will reach critical mass
If it fails
An empty community signals a dead product. The organic-acquisition flywheel never spins, and CAC stays high.
Mitigation
Seed with 50 beta travellers. Post-trip D+1 prompt at peak share intent. Treat utility (visa tips) as the hook, not social.
Validation: Measure D7 contribution rate in beta. Don't scale marketing until each new cohort produces self-sustaining UGC.
Medium Risk
Assumption: Users want one app, not best-of-breed
If it fails
Travellers keep cherry-picking specialists. The "complete the journey" thesis is wrong and retention craters.
Mitigation
Win one painful workflow (visa + rail) completely before bundling. Be genuinely better there, not just consolidated.
Validation: The 20-min "time to itinerary" metric is the kill-switch. If consolidation doesn't beat specialists on real tasks, rethink.

What Changed My Mind

Where research proved me wrong.The moments I had to rethink everything.

Strong opinions, loosely held. Each of these started as an assumption I was confident about, and an interview took it apart. That's the job.I was wrong about a lot. Here's where listening changed the product.

I believed
The core problem was booking, checkout UX, payment friction, conversion.
→
Research showed
Planning was the bigger pain. Travelers spent days deciding before they ever reached a checkout.
What I did: Repositioned the entire thesis from "a better OTA" to "the layer that reduces planning fragmentation." It reordered the whole roadmap.
I believed
Visa was a minor checkout-stage step, a document upload near the end.
→
Research showed
Visa anxiety was a top-of-funnel blocker. People hadn't booked because they feared rejection.
What I did: Moved Visa Intelligence from #4 to #2 and into Phase 1. It became an acquisition lever, not a feature.
I believed
An AI itinerary generator should be the flagship, the wow feature.
→
Research showed
Users wanted trust and consolidation first. A flashy generator on a fragmented base solves nothing.
What I did: Demoted AI Planner to a simple v1 and sequenced the generative version to Phase 2, behind the moat trio.
I believed
Community was a Phase 3 nice-to-have, a content layer to add once we had scale.
→
Research showed
Peer trust beat brand trust in every interview. UGC was a distribution engine, not decoration.
What I did: Kept the phasing but redesigned the cold-start: a post-trip D+1 prompt at peak share intent, seeded by 50 beta users.
How Glide Changed My Product Thinking

Growth, not a framework dump.How building this changed me.

Not a list of frameworks I know, a record of how my approach to product actually shifted.Each of these is a "before and after" in how I think.

Research
Talk before you build

Before, I trusted surveys and analytics to surface real problems. Research changed that, visa anxiety never appeared in any quantitative signal, only in a 20-minute conversation. Now I treat qualitative discovery as non-negotiable before a single line of PRD.Visa anxiety came from a 20-minute conversation, not a dashboard. Real problems don't always show up in data.

Prioritisation
Frameworks guide. Judgment decides.

Before, I let prioritisation frameworks make the decision for me. This project changed that, RICE ranked Visa #4, but moat analysis said #2, and the gap was a judgment call. Now I use frameworks to surface trade-offs, then own the decision myself.The spreadsheet gives you the ranking. Moving Visa AI to #2 took judgment, not a formula.

Scoping
No is the highest-leverage move

Before, I measured a roadmap by how much it included. Building this changed that, cutting 14 features before Sprint 1 made the product stronger, not weaker. Now I see scope as defined by what I reject, and "no" as my highest-leverage move.I cut 14 features before Sprint 1. The product is better because of what's not in it.

Supply Side
Partnerships are a PM problem

Before, I treated partnerships as someone else's problem, a BD concern, not a product one. This changed that, Amadeus (3 to 6mo) and JR East (4 to 8mo) timelines gate the entire roadmap. Now I sequence supply-side dependencies before Sprint 1, as a core PM responsibility.Getting rail APIs and visa data is harder than building the feature, and it's the PM's job to unblock it.

Community
Cold start has a fix

Before, I assumed community needed scale to work. This changed that, a post-trip D+1 prompt at peak share intent, seeded by just 50 users, beat any volume play. Now I design for the right moment, not the biggest audience.An empty community is worse than none. Post-trip is exactly the right moment, high emotion, high intent.

Measurement
Define "done" before you build

Before, I defined success metrics after shipping, once data came in. This changed that, features without a pre-defined KPI quietly became un-evaluable. Now every feature gets one KPI, one target and one threshold before it earns a sprint.If you can't answer "how do we know this worked?" before you start, stop. That's the question.
